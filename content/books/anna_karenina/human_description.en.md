# Stepan Arkadyich

> Stepan Arkadyich got dressed, sprayed himself with cologne, adjusted his shirt cuffs, and with practiced movements distributed his cigarettes, wallet, matches, and a double-chain watch with a pendant into his various pockets, then shook out his handkerchief. Despite his misfortune, he still felt clean, fragrant, physically healthy and full of vigor. He walked into the dining room with a slight spring in his step, where coffee was already laid out alongside letters and official papers from the ministry.

This passage comes at the very beginning of the book. Stepan's affair with the governess has just been discovered by his wife, who flew into a rage. This is Stepan's demeanor the morning after. As a high-ranking Russian official of the era, Stepan always maintained an air of refinement, propriety, and composure. Even when his inner world was in turmoil, the surface had to remain impeccable. The description is almost painfully true to life — so polished, so composed, so comfortable to read.

> He looked at the letters first. One from a merchant put him in a bad mood. The man wanted to buy a forest on his wife's estate. The forest certainly ought to be sold, but right now, before making peace with his wife, it was absolutely out of the question to discuss it. What particularly annoyed him was that this financial matter might become entangled with his impending marital reconciliation. Was he seeking reconciliation with his wife out of self-interest, just to sell that forest? The thought felt like an insult.

This passage deepens the portrait of his hypocrisy. He wants to sell his wife's property, yet feels that doing so wouldn't be dignified enough. In truth, his mind went to work, to assets — but never to his wife's feelings first. That is far more contemptible than simply sweet-talking his wife to close a land deal.

> Stepan Arkadyich subscribed to a liberal newspaper — not an extreme one, but the kind of liberalism favored by the majority. Although he had no real interest in science, art, or politics, he firmly held to the views of the majority and his newspaper on all three subjects, changing them only when the majority changed — or rather, he didn't change his views at all; they simply shifted inside his head without him noticing.

The liberal newspaper pushes the portrait even further: outwardly elegant, inwardly hollow. He didn't subscribe to understand national affairs or the people's suffering, but to stay current on the talking points of polite society — to avoid seeming out of touch. Whatever the paper said, that was his opinion. No free will, yet fully capable of pontificating on liberalism in front of others. The irony cuts deep.

> Having read the papers, finished his second cup of coffee, and eaten a buttered roll, he stood up, brushed the crumbs off his waistcoat, expanded his broad chest, and smiled cheerfully — not because he felt particularly cheerful, but because his digestion was working well.

The narcissism is breathtaking.

> The little girl, her father's favorite, ran in boldly, threw her arms around him, and hung from his neck laughing, delighting as always in the familiar scent of cologne from his sideburns. At last the girl kissed his face — flushed and tender from bending down — let go, and was about to run off, but her father held her back.
> "How is Mama?" he asked, stroking his daughter's soft, smooth neck. "Hello," he said with a smile to the boy who had come to greet him.
> He was conscious of not caring as much for his son, so he always tried to be fair; the boy sensed this and did not return his father's cool smile.

He has reached the point where even in front of his own children, he is performing.

> "Ah!" He lowered his head, and a sorrowful expression appeared on his handsome face. "To go or not to go?" he said to himself, while inside he was saying: no need to go, there would be nothing but pretense. Their relationship was beyond repair — because he could neither make her attractive enough to rekindle love, nor turn himself into an old man incapable of falling in love again. Nothing could come of it now except hypocrisy and lies, and hypocrisy and lies were against his nature.

This passage is almost absurdly realistic. How many real-life couples find themselves in relationships beyond repair — because "he could neither make her attractive enough to rekindle love, nor turn himself into an old man incapable of falling in love again" — yet remain bound together for the children's sake, or for financial interests, unable to divorce?

# Levin

> "No, wait, wait," he said. "You must understand — this is a matter of life and death for me. I have never spoken of this to anyone. And there is no one I can tell but you. You and I are completely different people in every way — tastes, views, everything. Nothing in common. But I know you like me and understand me, and that's why I'm so fond of you. For God's sake, be completely frank with me."

This passage truly resonated with me. There really are friends you grew up with — friends who are completely different from you in every possible way, in tastes, in views, in everything — and yet you know you genuinely like and understand each other. And some things can only be said to them.
