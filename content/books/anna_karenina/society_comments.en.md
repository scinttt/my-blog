# On Marriage Customs

> She noticed that social customs had changed greatly of late, and that a mother's responsibilities had grown heavier. She saw that girls of Kitty's age were forming societies of some sort, attending lectures, associating freely with men, driving through the streets unescorted. Many young women no longer curtsied, and most importantly, they all firmly believed that choosing a husband was their own affair, not their parents'. "Marrying off daughters isn't what it used to be," not only the young women but even the older generation thought and said. But how exactly one was supposed to marry off daughters today, the Princess could not discover. The French custom — where a daughter's fate was decided entirely by her parents — was no longer practiced and was openly condemned. The English custom — where the girl had complete autonomy — was also not followed, and in any case was unworkable in Russian society. The Russian custom of matchmaking through go-betweens was considered undignified and was ridiculed by everyone, including the Princess herself. So how, exactly, should young women marry? Nobody knew. Everyone who discussed it with the Princess said the same thing: "Come now, it's time to abandon the old ways. After all, it's the young people who are getting married, not their parents. Let them sort it out for themselves." Easy for them to say — these people had no daughters. The Princess understood perfectly well that when a daughter grew close to a man, she might fall in love. She might fall in love with a man who had no intention of marrying her, or with one entirely unsuitable as a husband. No matter how much people told her that in this day and age young people should arrange their own destinies, she refused to believe it — just as she would refuse to believe that the best toy for a five-year-old child was a loaded pistol.

Not much seems to have changed since then.
